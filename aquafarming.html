The Engineering Flow

A closed-loop masterpiece ensuring pristine water quality at every second.

01

Solid Removal

Water leaves the fish tanks and passes through Drum Filters (60 microns), instantly removing uneaten feed and waste particles. This rapid separation is crucial to prevent ammonia spikes and reduces biological load on downstream filters.

02

Bio-Filtration

Moving Bed Biofilm Reactors (MBBR) house billions of beneficial bacteria on specialized plastic carriers. These bacteria naturally convert toxic ammonia into harmless nitrates, mimicking the ocean's cleaning power in a compact footprint.

03

Degassing & Oxygenation

CO2 is stripped from the water in trickling towers, and pure oxygen is injected via mixing cones to maintain saturation levels above 100%. This hyper-oxygenated water drives rapid fish growth and improves feed conversion ratios.

Technical Deep Dive: The Science of RAS

Microbial Engine

The heart of any RAS is the biofilter. We utilize specific strains of nitrifying bacteria: Nitrosomonas to convert toxic Ammonia (NH3) into Nitrite (NO2-), and Nitrobacter to oxidize Nitrite into relatively harmless Nitrate (NO3-). Our bio-media provides 800m² of surface area per cubic meter of volume, maximizing bacterial colonies.

Critical Parameters

  • Dissolved Oxygen (DO): Maintained at >6mg/L for optimal metabolism.
  • pH Stability: Buffered automatically between 6.8 and 7.4.
  • Temperature: Species-specific control (e.g., 14°C for Salmon, 28°C for Tilapia).

Solids Management

Removing solid waste quickly is paramount. Our drum filters use microscopic mesh screens that automatically backwash based on pressure differential sensors. Removed sludge is thickened and anaerobically digested to produce biogas to power the facility, creating a secondary energy loop.

Biosecurity Protocols

Entering the facility requires a clean-room procedure: shower-in, dedicated clothing, and footbaths. Air is filtered via HEPA systems. This rigorous isolation means we never need to use vaccines or antibiotics commonly found in open-net farming.
